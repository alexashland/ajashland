Conferences

“‘figures cannot lie’: Documenting Novel Sources in Antebellum U.S. Literature,” Afterlives of Violence in American Literature and Culture, South Atlantic Modern Language Association (SAMLA), Birmingham, AL, Nov. 4, 2018.

This essay explores documentary literature as it emerged via the hybrid literary practices of the mid-nineteenth century. Turning to Harriet Beecher Stowe’s A Key to Uncle Tom’s Cabin, I interrogate the document’s attachments to both civic and religious discourse. Stowe recognized facts and figures not as neutral data or irrefutable fact, but rather as powerfully situated phenomena. Indeed, part of what makes her Key such an important (though still critically underexamined) text is her belief that documents are socially embedded, politically contingent products of social discourse. Her assemblage of tables, figures, and texts reveals the “textuality” of documentary curation. The failure of objectivity in documentary genres such as census data, legal testimonies, and congressional transcripts suggests, for Stowe, that the boundaries separating fact from more “literary” expressions are neither as useful nor as productive as they once seemed. Ultimately, I argue that Stowe's documentary strategies were meant to inspire action by exposing the mechanisms that traditional literature sought to conceal.

“Animating Whitman’s Correspondence: Pedagogy and Practice,” Walt Whtiman in/and the Digital Humanities, American Literature Association (ALA), Boston, MA, Nov. 27, 2017.

Animation and interactivity are common strategies used to visualize and communicate spatiotemporal phenomena, but scholars remain skeptical as to the advantages these techniques afford users. Research shows that, due to perceptual and cognitive limitations, animation and interactive design can actually interfere with comprehension and exploration of complex data. This paper examines how or if language could resolve some of these issues, and provide a more meaningful user experience. More specifically, I use data gathered from The Walt Whitman Archive to investigate the ways in which spatiotemporal phenomena and the complex systems that underlie them can benefit from incorporating existing scholarly narratives into their graphical display. Correspondence networks are extraordinarily complicated systems, and while it is useful to animate the trajectory and transmission of sent and received messages, much important information is lost in the process. My aim in this paper is partly to compare whether or not animated graphics are superior or inferior to informationally equivalent static graphics. I also suggest a series of best practices for digital projects wherein editorial complements could be used to enhance existing animated displays. I argue that narratives can improve users’ cognitive and perceptual awareness of social, historical, and contextual realities that remain implicit in data visualization. The overall goal of my DH project, Whitmap, aims to help users discover thematic and historical patterns that may otherwise have remained hidden due to the cognitive or perceptual limitations that come with animation-only design.
